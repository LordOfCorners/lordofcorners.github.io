A Journey Through Poland · September 2026

In Adam's Footsteps

Fifteen days across Poland — with a personal thread running through it

This itinerary covers fifteen days across Poland — Warsaw, Gdańsk, Łódź, Kraków, and the Tatra mountains. It's a trip built around great cities, good food, and personal exploration. Running through it is a family thread: My Journey: Memoirs of a Lucky Man, the memoir of Adam Kahane, who was born in Poland in 1922, survived deportation to Soviet labor camps, and emigrated to New York in 1948. Some of the places on this trip are places he lived. Others are just places worth going.

I. Warsaw Sep 4–7 · 3 Nights · Polonia Palace Hotel
Warsaw was 85% destroyed by the Nazis — deliberately, building by building — and rebuilt from photographs and paintings after the war. When Adam returned to Poland from the Soviet Union in January 1947, his train stopped a mile short of the ruined station. The city you walk through today is an act of collective memory.
Fri Sep 4
Arrive Warsaw · First Dinner in Poland

Arrive 1:00pm from JFK. Check in to Polonia Palace Hotel — a historic 4-star hotel that opened in 1913, Warsaw's second oldest, once frequented by diplomats and artists and where General Eisenhower stayed in September 1945. Walk the Old Town, rebuilt brick by brick from photographs after the Nazis destroyed 85% of the city. First dinner in Poland.

Sat Sep 5
POLIN Museum of the History of Polish Jews

One of the finest museums in Europe — a 1,000-year story told with extraordinary care. Plan at least 3 hours. Essential context for everything that follows: the Jewish world that existed in Poland before the war, its annihilation, and the handful of survivors.

From the memoir

"Of all the children shown in this picture, I am the only one left. Most did not survive the war."

— Adam Kahane, captioning a family photograph from Jasło
Sun Sep 6
Warsaw Uprising Museum · Praga · Nightlife

The Warsaw Uprising Museum tells the story of the 1944 uprising against Nazi occupation — what was happening in Poland while Adam and his family were surviving in Soviet labor camps and collective farms in Kazakhstan. Afternoon: cross the Vistula to Praga, Warsaw's bohemian neighbourhood. Evening: nightlife.

Mon Sep 7
Morning Free · Train to Gdańsk

Morning free. Train to Gdańsk departs 11:29am, arrives 2:01pm (2h 32m) — EIP 4506 high-speed service.

II. Gdańsk Sep 7–10 · 3 Nights · Gotyk House
Poland's great Baltic port city — a beautifully preserved Hanseatic old town, the birthplace of the Solidarity movement, and the city where WWII began when Germany invaded on September 1, 1939.
Mon Sep 7
Arrive Gdańsk · Old Town · Waterfront

Arrive 2:01pm. Check in to Gotyk House — located in the oldest tenement building in Gdańsk, dating to 1451, in the heart of the Old Town. Walk the Long Market (Długi Targ), the colourful Hanseatic waterfront, the medieval crane on the Motława river. Seafood dinner — Gdańsk is a Baltic port city and the fish is excellent.

Tue Sep 8
Malbork Castle Day Trip

One of the most impressive medieval castles in the world — a vast 13th-century Teutonic fortress about an hour by train from Gdańsk. Plan a full day.

Wed Sep 9
Possibly Sopot · European Solidarity Centre · Seafood Dinner

Optional morning trip to Sopot — 20 minutes by commuter train, a charming Belle Époque resort town with the longest wooden pier in Europe. Afternoon: the European Solidarity Centre, a superb museum telling the story of the Solidarity movement that brought down communism in Poland. Evening: one more seafood dinner on the Motława.

Thu Sep 10
Morning Free · Train to Łódź

Morning free in Gdańsk. Train departs 11:55am, arrives Łódź 4:00pm (4h 05m) — EIP 5352 direct service to Łódź Fabryczna station.

III. Łódź Sep 10–12 · 2 Nights · PURO Łódź
Adam's connection: He spent the first five years of his life here before his mother moved them to Jasło. His father Jacob ran a profitable pharmacy and pharmaceutical lab in Łódź. Adam returned here January–August 1947, running his father's pharmacy after the war. Jacob died on the train to Auschwitz from Łódź at age 54.
Thu Sep 10
Arrive Łódź · Piotrkowska Street · Dinner

Arrive 4:00pm at Łódź Fabryczna — a breathtaking modern station. Check in to PURO Łódź, steps from Manufaktura. Walk Piotrkowska Street, lined with Art Nouveau buildings stretching 4km through the city. Dinner and drinks.

From the memoir

"As I entered the pharmacy, I saw Uncle Iziu. He kissed me and said, 'This place is yours. I will help you run it for a while.'"

— Adam Kahane, on returning to Łódź in January 1947
Fri Sep 11
Jewish Cemetery · Off Piotrkowska · Manufaktura

The Łódź Jewish Cemetery is one of the largest in the world — over 180,000 graves, including the world's largest Jewish mausoleum. Adam's father Jacob operated his pharmacy in the Łódź ghetto almost until its final liquidation. In the afternoon, Off Piotrkowska — a once-abandoned factory now buzzing with cafés, bars and studios, identified by CNN as "Europe's hippest micro-scene." Evening at Manufaktura.

From the memoir

"My father stayed in Łódź during the occupation. Luckily his pharmacy was in the area designated as ghetto and he operated it almost to the time of the final liquidation of the ghetto. I was told by an Auschwitz survivor that he died of a heart attack on the train to the death camp. He was only 54."

— Adam Kahane
Sat Sep 12
Morning Wander · Train to Kraków

Morning wander — murals, woonerfs, the Museum of the City of Łódź in Poznański's Neo-Baroque palace. Train departs 1:26pm, arrives Kraków 4:34pm (3h 08m) — IC 8322.

IV. Kraków Sep 12–16 · 4 Nights · The Warszauer
Adam's connection: He lived at Świętego Sebastiana 5 — steps from Kazimierz, the historic Jewish quarter — from August 1947 until he left for Paris in September 1948. The Wistreich family (Reggie, Eva, Hugo, Aunt Hanka, Uncle Staszek) also lived in Kraków after returning from Kazakhstan. He visited again in 1976.
Sat Sep 12
Arrive Kraków · First Evening in Kazimierz

Arrive 4:34pm. Check in to The Warszauer — a 10-room boutique hotel in Kazimierz with a rooftop terrace overlooking the historic Jewish quarter. Walk to Świętego Sebastiana 5 — Adam's address from 1947 to 1948, a short walk from the hotel. Spend the evening in Kazimierz: synagogues, Jewish bookshops, restaurants, bars.

From the memoir

"We concluded that there is no future for us in communist Poland and decided to emigrate. The pharmacy was very profitable but I got only $2000 when I sold it... We left Poland in September 1947 after a year's stay."

— Adam Kahane, on leaving Kraków for Paris
Sun Sep 13
Wawel Castle · Schindler's Factory

Wawel Royal Castle in the morning — the historic seat of Polish kings above the Vistula. Book timed tickets online in advance

Schindler's Enamel Factory in the afternoon — a superb WWII museum telling the story of Kraków's Jews under Nazi occupation. Book tickets online — sells out

Mon Sep 14
Free Day — Possibly Jasło Day Trip

A free day — or a day trip to Jasło, about 2 hours by train. Jasło is Adam's hometown, where he grew up from age 5 to 17. The Rynek (market square) still has the house where he lived. The Jasiołka river where he and Reggie kayaked and baked potatoes in the ashes. Monastery Hill where he had his first kiss. The Boys' High School from which he was expelled just before the war began.

The Jasło Rynek in a prewar postcard.
Adam and Gert at the rebuilt family house on the Jasło Rynek, circa 1976. The ground floor had been converted to a state butcher's shop under communist Poland.
From the memoir

"On the other bank of the river were a meadow and a small grove. It was so pretty and peaceful there. Many years later, when I wanted to think about something relaxing, I thought about this place."

— Adam Kahane
Adam and friends on the Jasiołka river.

The weight of what happened in Jasło. The family that filled this town with life was almost entirely annihilated.

  • Uncle Dulek — a lawyer in Jasło. As he was marched to the train for Treblinka, he waved to his teacher Mr. Grudniewicz and called out in Latin: Ave Caesar, morituri te salutant — "Hail Caesar, we who are about to die salute you."
  • Aunt Manka — a military doctor decorated by the German Army in WWI. She believed her German citation would protect her. She was arrested by the Gestapo and shot. As she was led to her execution, she cursed her executioners and told them they would pay for it.
  • Cousins Renatka (21) and Jasia (18) — Manka's daughters, Adam's close companions. They were taken by car to a small town outside Jasło, ordered to push the car, and shot from behind. Jasia lived two more hours. Adam translated the account of their deaths for the Holocaust Museum.
Tue Sep 15
Free Day — Possibly Auschwitz and/or Wieliczka Salt Mine

A free day in Kraków, or one of two possible day trips:

Auschwitz-Birkenau — Adam's Uncle Mundek and his wife were taken here after fleeing Vienna. Adam's father Jacob almost certainly died on the train to this camp at age 54. A profound and deeply personal visit. Book guided tour weeks in advance at auschwitz.org

Wieliczka Salt Mine — a UNESCO World Heritage site 30 minutes from Kraków, with an underground cathedral carved entirely from salt. A completely different kind of day.

From the memoir — Uncle Mundek

"After his retirement they lived in Vienna. When Austria was annexed they moved to Holland — he was a Dutch citizen — and from there they were eventually taken to Auschwitz."

— Adam Kahane
Wed Sep 16
Morning Free · Train to Zakopane

Morning free in Kazimierz. Train departs 12:03pm, arrives Zakopane 2:13pm (2h 10m) — EIC 5350.

V. Zakopane Sep 16–18 · 2 Nights · Aries Hotel & SPA
Poland's mountain capital, gateway to the Tatras. Adam skied here as a boy and went on to run a successful ski shop in Washington D.C. for over 50 years — but you're here for the mountains, which are reason enough.
Wed Sep 16
Arrive Zakopane · Krupówki · Dinner

Arrive 2:13pm by train. Check in to Aries Hotel & SPA — centrally located, with thermal pool, outdoor jacuzzis, sauna, and breakfast included. Walk Krupówki, Zakopane's pedestrian street lined with stalls selling oscypek (smoked sheep's cheese), mountain food and crafts. The Tatra peaks rise dramatically on every side.

From the memoir

"In 1935 we took part in a sightseeing camp in the mountains. About 40 boys from all over Poland took part in the camp. We were the only Jewish boys... We hiked in high mountains and took a boat trip through a picturesque gorge. I will never forget how good the food tasted."

— Adam Kahane, on his mountain camp as a teenager
Adam and friends skiing near Jasło, 1930s.
Thu Sep 17
Morskie Oko Hike

The crown jewel of the Tatras — a glacial lake at 1,395 metres, surrounded by soaring peaks. Bus from Zakopane to the trailhead (Palenica Białczańska), then a 2-hour walk each way on a well-maintained path. The scenery is extraordinary. Return to the Aries for the thermal pool and sauna.

Fri Sep 18
Kasprowy Wierch Cable Car · Train to Kraków

Early start — cable car up Kasprowy Wierch (opens ~7:30am). Walk the ridge for as long as you like — even an hour at the top with those views is worth it. Return by cable car and check out. Train departs 1:58pm, arrives Kraków 3:57pm (1h 59m) — EIC 3550.

VI. Kraków — Farewell Sep 18–19 · 1 Night · PURO Kazimierz · Fly Sep 19
Fri Sep 18
Arrive from Zakopane · Final Evening in Kazimierz

Arrive Kraków 3:57pm. Check in to PURO Kazimierz. One final evening in the neighbourhood where Adam spent his last year in Poland before boarding the train to Paris in the autumn of 1947. Dinner, a drink, time to reflect on everything you've seen and felt.

From the memoir — on leaving Poland forever

"Finally, in the fall of 1947, we boarded the train to Paris with hopes and anxiety — to start a new chapter of our lives."

— Adam Kahane
Sat Sep 19
Fly Home

Morning free. Taxi to Kraków airport (~15 minutes). Fly home: LOT LO2083, departs 1:50pm, arrives Newark 5:50pm.

A Note on the Memoir

My Journey: Memoirs of a Lucky Man by Adam Kahane describes his childhood on the Jasło Rynek, his summers on the Jasiołka river, his deportation to Soviet labor camps, his postwar life in Łódź and Kraków, and his emigration to New York via Paris in 1948. He wrote it at the age of 90. Of the children in the family photograph from Jasło that appears in its pages, he was the only one who survived the war.

Some of the places on this trip are places he knew well. Some things are gone. Some, remarkably, are still there — including the house on the Rynek with the light red roof, the river on whose banks he spent his summers, and the mountains where he skied as a boy.

"Be optimistic. Eventually everything works out. Optimists live longer and enjoy life more. Hope you all will have as lucky and happy a life as I did."

— Adam Kahane, final words of his memoir, written at age 92
